where are we — shows the New Jersey municipality you are technically in, resolved against the state's official boundary polygons (NJOGIS "Municipal Boundaries of NJ", all 564 municipalities), and keeps a list of every town you've visited — with the municipality type, 2020 Census population, and land area from the same state layer. The zip line is your position tested against US Census ZCTA polygons: a close approximation of postal ZIP areas, not USPS gospel, shown with the postal place name (e.g. 07438 "Oak Ridge"), which in NJ often differs from the actual municipality. The school lines come from NJOGIS's financial-responsibility district boundaries (the district legally responsible for a resident student — reviewed annually with NJDOE) joined to the NJDOE public-school directory by district code; where an elementary and a regional high-school district differ, both are shown. "None on file" usually means a send/receive district whose students attend another district's high school by agreement. Multi-high-school townships list their schools — which one a given street attends is an attendance-zone question no public statewide layer answers. Population and area show the municipality's statewide rank among the 564, computed from the same layer, using the figures as the state publishes them. Legislative and congressional district layers are wired into the code but currently switched off. The map follows your dot; drag it to browse (following stops), and tap the big town name or the "follow" link to snap back. Population and area — with a statewide rank — now appear for every state covered, not just New Jersey: 2020 Census counts joined by GEOID, and land area computed the same way as NJ's. New York additionally carries a school district line, sourced from NYSED's own boundary service, and a best-effort high-school name where a text match succeeds — NY's schools data has no shared numeric code linking district polygons to school points the way NJ's does, so the join is by district name rather than by ID, and stays silent rather than claiming "none on file" when it can't find a match. Inside New York City the high-school line is skipped entirely: the city's ~32 geographic districts govern K-8 only, and high school admission is citywide choice, not tied to home address. The screen is kept awake automatically while the app is open and in view (supported browsers); switching away releases it immediately. Beyond NJ, nine states are covered — NY, NC, PA, CT, MA, NH, VT, ME, AZ — with Census TIGERweb boundaries (January 2025 vintage), downloaded lazily the first time you're there: in NY, towns and cities tile the state and villages sit on top of them, so a village address shows both (Connecticut's boroughs work the same way); PA and the New England states are fully tiled by their municipalities, unorganized territories included; in NC, most land is outside any municipality, and the app says "unincorporated [county]" because that is the technically correct answer; Arizona works the same way, and most of it is exactly that. Population outside NJ is 2020 Census counts joined onto the current boundaries by GEOID — a municipality whose boundaries changed since 2020 may show none. Connecticut's county line shows its planning regions — the state abolished county government, and those are its official county-equivalents. Inside New York City, a neighborhood line appears, sourced from the city's own 2020 Neighborhood Tabulation Areas — statistical polygons, not legal boundaries; the city itself cautions they aren't definitive neighborhood representations, which is why the line carries a [NYC statistical area] marker and neighborhoods are never recorded in the visited list. Parks, airports, and cemeteries drawn as their own tabulation areas are labeled "area" rather than "neighborhood." Outside-NJ readings carry a [census boundary] marker, show area with a statewide rank but no population yet, and skip the NJ-only school lines. The "cache" link in the footer shows exactly what is stored on this device, layer by layer. All layers download once and are cached on this device; after that, answers work offline. The map needs a connection for its base tiles; the boundary lines and your dot do not. Tap the big town name to center the map on yourself; tap a visited row to pan to that town. "days" counts distinct calendar days present. Outside New Jersey it reports coordinates only. Data: NJOGIS / NJGIN Open Data. Map tiles © OpenStreetMap contributors.
